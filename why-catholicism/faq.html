FAQ & Objections

Concise answers with Scripture, history, and pastoral notes. We aim for clarity and charity toward all Christians.

Sola Scriptura vs. Sacred Tradition

What the Church teaches

Divine Revelation is transmitted through Sacred Scripture and Sacred Tradition, interpreted authentically by the Magisterium (Dei Verbum 9–10).

Scriptural & historical basis

  • Acts 15 shows the apostles and elders judging a doctrinal dispute.
  • 2 Thess 2:15 exhorts believers to stand firm and hold to traditions, whether by word of mouth or letter.
  • Early Fathers (Irenaeus) appeal to apostolic succession to preserve true teaching.

Pastoral note

Scripture remains central; Tradition safeguards its right interpretation within the living Church.

Biblical basis for the papacy and primacy

What the Church teaches

Peter receives a unique pastoral office serving unity; the Bishop of Rome, as Peter’s successor, exercises a primacy of love and jurisdiction.

Scriptural & historical basis

  • Mt 16:18–19: “You are Peter… I will give you the keys…”
  • Jn 21:15–17: “Feed my sheep.”
  • Acts 1–15: Peter’s distinctive role in teaching and governance.
  • Fathers (Irenaeus) regard the Roman Church as a normative reference.

Pastoral note

Papal primacy serves unity and truth, not domination; it is exercised collegially with the bishops.

Are Catholic doctrines later inventions?

What the Church teaches

Doctrine develops organically—clarifying what is implicit in the apostolic deposit—without contradicting earlier teaching (cf. Newman’s development of doctrine).

Scriptural & historical basis

  • Councils articulate doctrine in response to new questions (e.g., Trinity, Christology).
  • Continuity is measured by identity of faith, worship, and governance.

Pastoral note

Development helps the Church speak clearly to each age while remaining faithful to Christ’s teaching.

Veneration of Mary and the saints vs. worship

What the Church teaches

Worship (latria) belongs to God alone; veneration (dulia) honors saints; Mary receives special veneration (hyperdulia) because of her unique relation to Christ.

Scriptural & historical basis

  • Lk 1:48: “All generations will call me blessed.”
  • Ancient Christian practice of asking for the prayers of martyrs and saints.

Pastoral note

Rightly understood, Marian and saintly devotion directs us to Jesus and imitates their faith.

Eucharist: symbolic or Real Presence?

What the Church teaches

Christ is truly, really, substantially present under the appearances of bread and wine; the Mass makes present His one sacrifice.

Scriptural & historical basis

  • Jn 6; 1 Cor 10:16; Last Supper narratives.
  • Ignatius and Justin testify to early belief in the Real Presence.

Pastoral note

The Eucharist is the source and summit of Christian life, inviting deeper communion and charity.

Justification, faith, and works

What the Church teaches

We are justified by grace through faith; this faith is living and expresses itself in love and obedience (Gal 5:6).

Scriptural & historical basis

  • Rom 3–5; James 2; Council of Trent’s Decree on Justification.

Pastoral note

Salvation is a gift; our cooperation is itself empowered by grace.

Why Catholic and not Orthodox or Protestant?

What the Church teaches

The Catholic Church possesses the fullness of the means of salvation and a visible universal unity centered on the successor of Peter.

Scriptural & historical basis

  • Visible unity and communion across time and place; apostolic succession linked to Rome from early centuries.
  • Shared sacraments and much doctrine with Orthodox; significant real, though imperfect, communion with many Protestants.

Pastoral note

We honor the gifts present in other communities and pray for full visible unity in truth and charity.
