Apostolic History

From the Apostles to the present, the Catholic Church preserves the deposit of faith through apostolic succession, councils, and the Petrine ministry.

Timeline & Milestones

  • AD 30–100

    Apostles and early bishops

    The Apostles appoint bishops and presbyters; local churches celebrate the Eucharist and baptism; the New Testament is being written.

  • AD 440–461

    Pope Leo I (the Great)

    Leo’s pastoral and doctrinal leadership (e.g., the Tome of Leo) articulates Petrine ministry in service of unity; received at Chalcedon (451).

  • 2nd–3rd c.

    Canon formation and catechesis

    Lists of sacred books emerge; liturgy and catechumenate develop; Fathers witness to episcopal leadership and the Eucharist.

  • AD 325

    Council of Nicaea

    Affirms the Son as consubstantial with the Father; produces a creed that remains central in Catholic worship.

  • 381–451

    Constantinople, Ephesus, Chalcedon

    Clarify Trinitarian and Christological doctrine; articulate the unity of Christ’s person with two natures.

  • 1545–1563

    Council of Trent

    Reaffirms sacramental theology, the canon, and the role of Tradition in response to Reformation controversies.

  • 1870

    Vatican I

    Defines papal primacy and infallibility under specific conditions; situates teaching authority within episcopal college.

  • 1854 • 1950

    Doctrinal definitions (Pius IX; Pius XII)

    Ineffabilis Deus (Immaculate Conception, 1854) and Munificentissimus Deus (Assumption, 1950) exemplify papal definitions tied to Scripture and Tradition.

  • 1962–1965

    Vatican II

    Deepens teaching on Revelation, liturgy, and the Church’s mission; affirms Scripture and Tradition with the Magisterium.

Early Church Voices

Ignatius of Antioch (c. AD 35–108)

“Let no one do anything of concern to the Church without the bishop.”

— Letter to the Smyrnaeans 8

Irenaeus of Lyons (c. AD 130–202)

“It is a matter of necessity that every Church should agree with this Church [of Rome].”

— Against Heresies 3.3.2

Augustine of Hippo (AD 354–430)

“I would not believe the Gospel unless moved by the authority of the Catholic Church.”

— Contra epistolam Manichaei 5.6

Doctrinal Continuity: Witness and Teaching

Key topics with Catholic teaching, early witnesses, and notes
Topic Catholic Position Early Witnesses / Texts Notes
Authority Scripture and Tradition form one sacred deposit, interpreted by the Magisterium. Acts 15; Dei Verbum; Irenaeus, Against Heresies. Council decisions exemplify authoritative discernment.
Eucharist Real Presence of Christ; sacrificial memorial. 1 Cor 10–11; Ignatius, Philadelphians 4; Justin Martyr, Apology I.66. Continuity of belief and practice across centuries.
Baptism New birth by water and Spirit; incorporation into the Church. John 3:5; Didache 7; Tertullian, On Baptism. Infant baptism attested early in many regions.
Justification & Grace By grace through faith working in love; cooperation with grace. James 2; Augustine; Council of Trent (Decree on Justification). Faith is living and active through charity.
Communion of Saints The faithful on earth united with the saints and souls in purgation. Heb 12:1; inscriptions and prayers in catacombs. Intercession reflects the Body of Christ’s unity.
Marian Dogmas Theotokos; perpetual virginity; Immaculate Conception; Assumption. Ephesus (431); Later Fathers; papal definitions (1854, 1950). Christological clarity drives Marian doctrine.
Canon of Scripture OT and NT canon received in the Church’s life and liturgy. Local councils (Hippo 393; Carthage 397/419); Trent (1546). Canon lists align with liturgical use and tradition.
